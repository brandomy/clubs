# Executive Summary: The Case for Early Wheelchair Intervention in Children Ages 2-5

**Prepared for:** HuaQiao Foundation  
**Source Report:** Early Wheelchair Intervention Research Brief  
**Date:** December 2025

---

## Part 1: Narrative Summary

### Key Finding

Children with mobility impairments in ASEAN face a preventable developmental catastrophe due to lack of early wheelchair access. With 90% of brain development occurring by age 5 and neural plasticity highest in the first three years, delaying wheelchair provision causes permanent cognitive, physical, and social harm that early intervention could largely prevent. The economic return is compelling: $15-38 for every dollar invested.

### Context

This research directly supports HuaQiao Foundation's strategic positioning in the early childhood disability space. The foundation's existing Gift of Mobility program (7,850 wheelchairs donated, USD $500k+) demonstrates operational capacity, while its Rotary partnerships provide the international network needed for ASEAN expansion. No government in the region currently targets the 2-5 age cohort systematically—creating an opportunity for HuaQiao to own this cause for a generation.

The research aligns with HuaQiao's bridge-building mission: connecting international medical resources with underserved communities, building local capacity, and demonstrating what early intervention can accomplish. This is precisely the type of innovative, pragmatic, and sustainable project that advances social development as defined in the foundation's mission statement.

### Top Recommendations (Prioritized)

1. **Launch pilot program in Vietnam or Cambodia** — Both have existing Wheelchairs for Kids distribution networks and demonstrated NGO success (Saigonchildren). Start with 50-100 children ages 2-5 to establish baseline data that no ASEAN government currently collects.

2. **Partner with Rotary District 3350 (Thailand)** — WHO wheelchair training programs have been piloted in Bangkok. Leverage existing Rotary relationships to access clinical training infrastructure and potential co-funding.

3. **Develop age-specific screening protocol** — Work with pediatric partners to identify children at GMFCS Levels IV-V (96% will remain wheelchair users into adulthood) during the critical 2-5 window before hip displacement begins.

4. **Incorporate Go Baby Go as entry-level option** — At ~$200 per modified ride-on car versus $280-300 for standard wheelchair, this provides accessible early mobility for children as young as 7 months while building toward permanent wheelchair provision.

5. **Document outcomes for advocacy** — Track developmental gains, prevented complications, and caregiver impact to build the evidence base that can influence ASEAN regional policy and attract government co-investment.

### Critical Caveats

**Data Gaps:**
- No ASEAN country tracks children ages 2-5 needing wheelchairs — prevalence estimates must be extrapolated from global data
- Only 50% of LMIC countries and 15% of datasets meet international disability measurement standards
- Lifetime cost data ($921k+) comes from US CDC; LMIC-specific economic burden data limited to one Uganda study

**Confidence Issues:**
- Gold-standard RCT evidence (Jones et al. 2012) used children 14-30 months in US context; ASEAN replication not yet done
- Cultural stigma varies significantly across ASEAN nations; Vietnam success may not predict Cambodia/Philippines outcomes
- Hip displacement rates (30-90%) come from clinical populations, may overstate or understate community-level need

**Verify Before External Presentation:**
- Current wheelchair access rates in target countries (3% figure is global low-income average)
- Unit costs from Wheelchairs for Kids for specific ASEAN delivery points
- Local partner organization capacities and co-funding potential
