# ASEAN B40 Actual Median Income: Correcting the Wheelchair Affordability Analysis

Using B40 income thresholds rather than actual medians overstates purchasing power by **25-40%** across ASEAN countries. Malaysia's often-cited B40 "income" of RM 5,249 is the ceiling to qualify—actual B40 median is **RM 3,440**, reducing estimated affordability substantially. When disability-related income penalties (4-6 percentage points higher poverty) and extra costs (12-40% of income) are factored in, wheelchair affordability gaps widen dramatically, particularly in lower-income ASEAN nations where a $300 wheelchair represents **1-4+ months** of total household income.

## The threshold-versus-median discrepancy significantly affects analysis

The distinction between B40 thresholds and actual medians matters enormously for affordability calculations. In Malaysia—where this methodology is best documented—the B40 threshold ceiling is RM 5,249, but actual B40 median income sits at **RM 3,440**, approximately 35% lower. This pattern likely holds across ASEAN countries using similar classification schemes.

For Indonesia, the methodology differs entirely: BPS uses consumption-based expenditure classes rather than income. This creates additional complexity because approximately **48.5%** of Indonesian households fall into "Class 3" (near-middle class), and the B40 population spans Classes 1, 2, and lower portions of Class 3. Direct income figures require estimation from expenditure distributions.

Vietnam and Cambodia present similar measurement challenges. Neither country publishes direct B40 median income figures—researchers must calculate estimates from quintile averages in household surveys. The median within the bottom 40% typically falls below the simple average of quintiles 1 and 2 due to income inequality, meaning even these estimates may overstate typical earnings.

## Disability compounds poverty through multiple mechanisms

Research across ASEAN reveals a consistent **4-6 percentage point premium** in poverty rates for households with disabled members. In Thailand, households with PWD experience poverty rates 5.9 percentage points higher than the general population; in Indonesia, the gap is 4.3 percentage points.

Beyond higher poverty incidence, disability imposes substantial **extra costs** that reduce effective purchasing power:

- **Vietnam**: 12% of household income consumed by disability-related costs, plus $595/year in additional health expenditures alone
- **Indonesia**: Households with moderate-severe disabilities face up to 20% additional per-capita costs
- **Global average for developing countries**: Extra costs range from $1,170 to $6,952 annually, representing 12-40% of household income

When poverty lines are adjusted for these extra costs, **1 in 4** Indonesian households with a disabled member fall below poverty—compared to 1 in 12 households without disability. This "conversion handicap" (Amartya Sen's term) means families with disabled children effectively have less disposable income than monetary figures suggest.

Children with disabilities face compounded disadvantages: they are **nearly 3x more likely** to experience poverty than peers without disabilities, 50% less likely to attend school, and 25% less likely to receive medical care. In ASEAN specifically, enrollment drops **52.7%** between primary and secondary education for children with disabilities, limiting future earning potential.

## Rural populations face disproportionate barriers

Rural residence correlates strongly with both disability and poverty across ASEAN. In Thailand, **73.2%** of the lowest-income households are in rural areas, predominantly in the Northeastern region. Cambodia's rural population (61% of total) experiences concentrated poverty, with provincial incomes significantly lower than Phnom Penh. Vietnam shows similar patterns: rural household income averages VND 4.5 million/month versus VND 6.9 million/month in urban areas—a **35% gap**.

Rural B40 income estimates consistently fall **24-40% below** national B40 medians:

- **Thailand**: Rural B40 median ~$229-286/month vs national ~$343
- **Indonesia**: Rural B40 ~$222/month vs national ~$285
- **Malaysia**: Rural median ~$991/month overall, with B40 rural households likely around $497/month
- **Vietnam**: Rural B40 ~$73-90/month per capita vs urban B40 ~$82/month per capita

For families needing pediatric wheelchairs—who are disproportionately rural and already experience disability-related income reductions—effective purchasing power may be **40-60% lower** than national B40 figures suggest.

## Data limitations and confidence assessment

**High confidence countries** (Malaysia, Philippines, Singapore): Official national statistics offices publish detailed income distribution data by decile, allowing precise B40 median calculations. Data is recent (2022-2023) and methodologically robust.

**Medium confidence countries** (Thailand, Vietnam, Indonesia, Brunei, Cambodia): Data exists but requires estimation from quintile averages or expenditure proxies. Vietnam and Cambodia lack direct B40 median figures; Indonesia uses consumption rather than income measurement. Brunei's most recent comprehensive household survey dates to 2015.

**Low confidence countries** (Myanmar, Laos): Myanmar faces severe data constraints post-2021 due to political instability—UNDP relies on phone surveys with significant selection bias. Laos data (LECS 2019) predates both COVID-19 and substantial currency depreciation (60% since survey). Both countries' extreme currency volatility makes USD conversions unreliable.

**Cross-cutting limitation**: No ASEAN country systematically disaggregates household income data by disability status in published statistics. Disability-income correlations come from academic studies and international organizations rather than official surveys.

## Conclusion: Recalibrating intervention strategies

The corrected B40 median incomes reveal that previous analyses using threshold values **understated affordability barriers by 25-40%**. For the seven lower-income ASEAN countries (excluding Singapore, Brunei, and Malaysia), a $300 pediatric wheelchair represents approximately **one full month to over four months** of total B40 household income.

**Highest-priority intervention countries**: Myanmar and Laos emerge as critical—wheelchair costs exceed four months of B40 household income, and data suggests conditions are deteriorating. Cambodia and Indonesia follow closely, with costs exceeding one month's income even before accounting for rural and disability penalties.

**Key recalibration factors** for foundation prioritization:
1. Use actual B40 medians, not thresholds—reduces assumed purchasing power by ~35%
2. Apply rural discount of 24-40% for families most likely to need wheelchairs
3. Factor disability premium of 4-6 percentage points higher poverty plus 12-20% extra costs
4. Recognize that lowest-income households consistently spend more than they earn (structural deficit), making any large purchase essentially impossible without subsidies

For a rural B40 family with a disabled child in Indonesia, the effective purchasing power may be as low as **$150-180/month** once all adjustments are applied—making a $300 wheelchair equivalent to nearly **two months of total household income**. Without direct subsidy, payment plans, or refurbished equipment programs, wheelchair access for these families remains functionally impossible.
